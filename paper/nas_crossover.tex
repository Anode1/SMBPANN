\begin{abstract}
Evolutionary neural architecture search recombines architectures with crossover, but a
recurring result is that random search is hard to beat, and it is rarely clear whether
crossover contributes anything of its own. We study this directly on NAS-Bench-101, a
real, tabulated space of $423{,}624$ cell architectures whose fitness is a lookup, so a
run is microseconds and thousands of paired seeds are affordable. Two findings, both
reported with paired significance tests. First, on this space \emph{crossover does beat
random search}, significantly and at every compute budget. Second, and against
intuition, \emph{making the crossover smarter makes it worse}. The NAS-Bench-101 cell is
naturally multi-dimensional --- a wiring dimension (the adjacency) and a labeling
dimension (the operations), plus a graph/path view --- and it is tempting to recombine
along those dimensions: factor wiring from labeling, align nodes by role before
recombining, or transplant whole input--output paths. Every such structured operator
underperforms plain per-cell uniform crossover; two of them, at large budget, drop
\emph{below random search itself}. We trace this to diversity: the structural priors
mismanage the offspring variety that crossover-plus-selection needs --- collapsing it into
premature convergence (axis, path) or maintaining it without exploiting it (aligned) ---
while plain uniform crossover balances exploration against exploitation. The lesson
restates a known one ---
random search is a strong NAS baseline, and structural cleverness rarely beats a simple
baseline --- now with a controlled mechanism on a real space. The fitness oracle,
isomorphism-correct by brute-force canonicalization, and every operator are a single
dependency-free C program; every number regenerates from one command.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Neural architecture search (NAS) automates the choice of a network's shape; methods
differ mainly in the search strategy --- reinforcement learning \cite{zoph2017},
gradient relaxation \cite{darts2019}, Bayesian optimization, random search, and
evolution \cite{elsken2019}. Evolutionary NAS mutates and recombines architectures and
trains each candidate's weights by back-propagation \cite{real2019}. A recurring and
sobering result is that plain random search is a strong baseline \cite{bergstra2012,li2019},
and within evolutionary methods it is often unclear whether \emph{crossover} earns its
place: the field's leading evolutionary result drops crossover entirely and keeps only
mutation with aging \cite{real2019}.

We ask two questions and answer them on a real benchmark. Does crossover beat random
search at all? And if the search space has structure --- NAS-Bench-101's cell factors
into a wiring dimension and a labeling dimension --- does a crossover that \emph{respects
that structure} beat one that ignores it? The intuition that it should is strong: a
graph's building blocks are its subgraphs and its wiring, not arbitrary bits, and
recombining along the natural axes ought to preserve what makes a parent good. We find
the first answer is yes and the second is no --- emphatically, and reversing at scale.

The engine behind this work is a self-modifying back-propagation network whose topology
and training hyper-parameters co-evolve \cite{thesis1997}; here we use only its
recombination logic, and to keep the comparison clean we move fitness onto the
NAS-Bench-101 table so no confound of training noise enters.

\section{The space and the fitness oracle}

A NAS-Bench-101 cell is a directed acyclic graph on up to seven nodes: node~0 is the
input, node~$n{-}1$ the output, and the interior nodes carry one of three operations
(\texttt{conv1x1}, \texttt{conv3x3}, \texttt{maxpool3x3}). An architecture thus has two
orthogonal dimensions, its \emph{wiring} (the adjacency matrix) and its \emph{labeling}
(the interior operations), and a third, derived view as a set of input--output paths.
The public benchmark records the trained CIFAR-10 accuracy of every one of the
$423{,}624$ distinct cells, so fitness is a table lookup and no network is trained
during search.

The one subtlety is that the benchmark de-duplicates \emph{isomorphic} graphs: the same
abstract cell can be written with its interior nodes permuted, and a recombined child
will frequently be a relabeling of a catalogued cell rather than a verbatim match.
Looking a child up therefore requires a canonical form. We compute it by brute force:
prune the nodes that lie on no input--output path, then take the lexicographically
minimum adjacency-plus-labeling encoding over all $\le 5! = 120$ permutations of the
interior nodes. This is exact and dependency-free. A self-test confirms it: over
$5{,}000$ catalogued cells, both the verbatim graph and a random interior relabeling of
it map to the same accuracy, $5000/5000$ each, and the $423{,}624$ table rows collapse to
exactly $423{,}624$ distinct canonical keys (no collisions). The same canonicalization
supplies the node correspondence used by the alignment operator below.

The data itself is extracted from the official \texttt{nasbench\_only108.tfrecord} by a
small C decoder (TFRecord framing, JSON, and a hand-parsed \texttt{ModelMetrics}
protobuf); best test accuracy $94.32\%$ and best validation $95.06\%$ reproduce the
published optima.

\section{Operators}

Every individual is a seven-node cell in upper-triangular form (input $0$, output $6$).
All arms share the same population loop, the same light mutation (flip one edge or
re-roll one interior op), the same elitism, and the same matched query budget; \emph{the
arms differ only in the crossover operator}, so any difference is attributable to it.
Selection is on validation accuracy and we report test accuracy, so nothing overfits the
reported metric. We compare four crossovers against a random-search control:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{flat} --- per-cell uniform crossover: each adjacency bit and each op is
copied from a uniformly chosen parent. It ignores the graph structure entirely; it is the
maximum-diversity baseline (and the ``competing conventions'' strawman, since node $k$ in
one parent need not play the same role as node $k$ in the other).
\item \textbf{axis} --- dimension-factored: the whole wiring is taken from one parent and
the whole labeling from the other. This recombines the two dimensions as units.
\item \textbf{aligned} --- role-aligned: match the interior nodes of the second parent to
the first by role (operation and in/out degree), bringing it into the first parent's
coordinate frame, then recombine \emph{node modules} --- each node's whole incoming-edge
set and its op come together from one aligned parent. This removes the competing-conventions
problem before recombining.
\item \textbf{path} --- subgraph transplant: a cell is a set of input--output paths;
recombine whole paths from both parents, each path carrying its own parent's ops, added
greedily under the nine-edge cap. This recombines along the graph dimension.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Matched compute and pairing.} Each arm may query the oracle a fixed number of
times; the random control spends the same number of queries on independent random cells;
a child that prunes to an invalid or over-budget graph is a spent query scored zero, so an
operator that wastes queries on invalid graphs is penalized honestly. Crucially, in each
trial the control and all GA arms are given the \emph{same} seed, so they start from an
identical initial population; a trial is thus a paired observation, and we report both the
paired win/loss counts and the Wilcoxon signed-rank $z$ of (arm $-$ baseline).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Crossover beats random search}

At a small population ($20$, elite $5$, $800$ seeds), every crossover arm beats random
search significantly at every budget (Wilcoxon $z$ from $2$ to $14$; win rates well above
one half). Unlike the tiny six-gene NAS-Bench-201 cell, where crossover's edge over random
is a fraction of a percent and vanishes at any generous budget, the larger NAS-Bench-101
space gives recombination something real to exploit. So the first question is settled in
the affirmative: crossover helps here.

\subsection{Structure does not help --- and at scale it hurts}

The second question is the substance. Table~\ref{tab:scaled} scales the population to $50$
(elite $12$) and the budget to $20{,}000$ evaluations, $600$ paired seeds. The ordering is
stark and monotone. Plain \textbf{flat} crossover is the best at \emph{every} budget. The
structured operators --- the ones that respect the space's dimensions --- are worse, and
worsen as compute grows: the dimension-factored \textbf{axis} operator falls \emph{below
random search} at the two larger budgets (a significantly negative $z$), and the aligned
operator's advantage over random decays to nothing.

The trends are clean and monotone across the four budgets. Flat's advantage over random
shrinks as random saturates (its $z$ falls $19\!\to\!18\!\to\!12\!\to\!6$) but never
disappears; it beats every structured operator head-to-head at every budget. Axis, which
factors the two dimensions into large indivisible units, converges prematurely and its $z$
against random crosses from $+11$ to $-11$; by $20{,}000$ evaluations it loses to random
search $176/399$. The path operator behaves the same way and more starkly: its mean best
accuracy barely moves across a fortyfold increase in budget ($93.922 \to 93.930 \to 93.933
\to 93.934$) --- it has essentially converged by $500$ evaluations and then stops --- so
random search sails past it and it too ends significantly \emph{below} random ($z=-8.0$).
Only the aligned operator, which mixes at the intermediate granularity of node modules,
holds on: its edge over random decays from $z=16$ to $z=0.9$, exactly to the break-even
point. So of the four crossovers, the one that ignores structure wins at every budget, the
two most structured ones (axis, path) fall below random search at scale, and the
intermediate one ends in a tie with it.

The obvious escape --- that the structured operators fail because they produce more
invalid graphs --- is closed by measurement: axis, aligned, and path in fact produce
\emph{more} valid offspring ($95$--$97\%$) than flat ($90$--$96\%$), so flat wins on
genuine search quality, not by dodging invalid graphs.

\subsection{The mechanism is diversity}

Two lines of evidence bear on the cause, and together they refine it from raw diversity to
\emph{productive} diversity. First, measuring the final population's genotypic spread (mean
pairwise gene distance over the $21$ edges and $5$ ops) exposes two distinct failure modes.
The two operators that end below random search, axis and path, are exactly the two that
\emph{collapse} that spread (final diversity $1.8$ and $1.3$ against flat's $2.1$ at
$20{,}000$ evaluations): they converge prematurely, which is precisely the plateau their
accuracy curves show. But diversity alone is not the whole story --- the aligned operator
maintains the \emph{most} spread of all ($3.1$, above flat) and still only ties random,
because its structural recombination keeps the population varied without concentrating it on
good cells. Flat holds an intermediate, productive spread and wins. So the structured
operators fail in one of two ways --- by collapsing diversity (axis, path) or by spreading it
unproductively (aligned) --- and only the structure-blind operator balances exploration
against exploitation. (We report this rather than the cleaner ``diversity ranks the
operators'' story we first expected; the measurement does not support that.)

Second, and independently, varying the \emph{population} size at fixed budget sweeps the
diversity \emph{available} to crossover, and the whole comparison moves with it
(Table~\ref{tab:pop}). At a tiny population of eight, crossover has too little variety to work
with and \emph{every} arm, flat included, loses to random search ($z<0$). As the population
grows the crossover gain climbs monotonically for every operator, until at population $64$
all four beat random decisively. Crossover on this space is not intrinsically better or worse
than random search; it is better exactly when it has enough diversity to draw on and an
operator that turns diversity into good offspring rather than dissipating or collapsing it.

So crossover on this space works because fine-grained mixing plus selection keeps proposing
varied candidates near good regions; every structural prior we add trades that variety for a
coherence the space does not reward, and the more compute we spend, the more the low-diversity
operators converge early and are overtaken by random search, which never stops exploring.

\section{Relation to prior work}

The framing is not new and we do not claim it is. Random search as a strong NAS baseline is
\cite{bergstra2012,li2019}; mutation-only evolutionary NAS that deliberately omits crossover
is \cite{real2019}; the difficulty of meaningful crossover on graph genomes --- the
competing-conventions problem that our aligned operator is designed to remove --- is the
motivation of NEAT's historical markings \cite{neat2002}. NAS-Bench-101 \cite{nasbench101}
is the tabulated space that makes a study at this seed count possible. Our contribution is
not a method but a clean, controlled, reproducible \emph{negative} result: on a real
multi-dimensional NAS space, four different structure-respecting crossovers all lose to
plain uniform crossover, two of them to random search, with a diversity mechanism that
explains why.

\section{Discussion and conclusion}

On NAS-Bench-101 crossover beats random search --- a genuine positive, larger and cleaner
than the same comparison on the smaller NAS-Bench-201 cell. But the appealing idea that a
crossover should recombine along the search space's natural dimensions does not survive
contact with the data: factoring the wiring and labeling dimensions, aligning nodes by role,
and transplanting whole paths all underperform a structure-blind uniform crossover, and the
more structured the operator the sooner it is overtaken by random search as the budget grows.
The cause is diversity, not validity: structural priors narrow the offspring distribution
that crossover-plus-selection depends on. \textbf{Limitations:} results are on one tabulated
cell space with a table-lookup fitness (no training-time confounds, but also no transfer to
larger or hierarchical spaces); the aligned operator is one reasonable alignment and a better
one may exist, though the burden is now on exhibiting one that beats uniform crossover, which
four natural attempts do not. The natural next step is a larger, hierarchical space where
recombination has more room to matter.

\section*{Reproducibility}

Every number regenerates from \cite{repo} with one command. The fitness table is extracted
from the official benchmark by \texttt{nb101\_extract.c} (\texttt{make nb101\_extract});
the search, oracle, and all operators are \texttt{validation/nb101.c} (\texttt{make nb101}),
configurable by the environment (\texttt{POP}, \texttt{ELITE}, \texttt{SEEDS},
\texttt{BUDGETS}) and self-testing its oracle with \texttt{--selftest}. Both build clean
under \texttt{-std=c99 -pedantic} and run clean under AddressSanitizer and UBSan. A fixed
xorshift generator makes every run reproducible.
