In 1997 I did my homework: derived the multi-layer delta rule for a seminar, drew the diagrams in MS Paint, and closed with a handful of heuristics. My professor liked it enough to offer me grad school. I said no, I had a move to Canada and a healthy instinct for avoiding citations.

The real idea showed up later, by accident and on the clock. In 2001 I was still pitching the thesis, backprop steered by those heuristics, to anyone who would listen. Then in 2002, while building a genetic algorithm for an actual industrial optimization problem, for money, on a deadline, the thought finally landed: don't hand the network its shape at all; hand it the problem and the target error, and let it find its own. Without meaning to, I had sketched the next fifteen years of a field. I watched evolution beat the brute-force baseline, and the bet was born: evolution beats random. Then life took over, and the idea waited in a drawer for two decades while I did the honorable thing, shipping software nobody will cite either.

This year I rebuilt it in clean C, watched it discover convolution all by itself, felt briefly like a genius, then opened the literature and found NEAT, DeepMind, Real et al., and LEMONADE had all published the contents of my drawer, right on schedule, without asking.

To be fair to my younger self, the bet was a good one. Back in 2002, on a real problem for real money, evolution beat brute force outright, that win was real. When I finally put it on a proper benchmark this year, though, it shrank: across thousands of paired runs crossover does edge random, consistently and in the right direction, but by a margin so thin it sits inside the benchmark's own noise. I was right the way a coin that lands heads 50.3 percent of the time proves you right, the direction held, the magnitude filed for bankruptcy.

My mistake was the sequel. I decided to be clever about it, to recombine along the architecture's structure, and my clever operators promptly lost to the dumb one, and then to random search itself. A crisp, well-controlled way to rediscover that nobody had asked me to be clever.

This year's coda was a second run at it, from the opposite end. Instead of searching for the architecture I tried to *grow* one, start from an exhaustive tangle, put a price on every wire, and watch which parts of convolution survive the squeeze. Some do: the thing finds which inputs matter, and switches on weight-sharing when translation pays for it. One part refuses to come, the tight little filter, and this time I can say in a single line exactly why, which is the closest I have come to a real result. To keep myself honest I turned three machine scientists loose on the code; they found no bug and handed back the same verdict twice: the map is honest and mostly things the field already knows, and the one new move is having grown the structure from the exhaustive end instead of the minimal one, negatives written down and all. What did *not* emerge, it turns out, is the part worth keeping.

So: right, early, not first. The bet came in, evolution does edge random, but by a hair too fine to spend, at once a vindication and a punchline. The thesis has a DOI now, a tombstone with a timestamp. I'm going to make dinner, the one architecture I can still claim as my own.
