\begin{abstract}
Ordinarily a neural network is handed its shape before it learns; the system here is
handed only the data and a target error and evolves the rest, its depth and width,
whether each layer is dense or convolutional, and its own learning rate, momentum, and
activation. Onto that mutation-driven search we add \emph{crossover}, and ask, across
four small controlled experiments, when recombining two parents beats mutating one.
The answer is a single rule. On concatenated deceptive trap functions, where the
solution splits into independent blocks, our crossover beats mutation decisively and
its advantage grows with the number of blocks. On the neural architecture search it
does nothing: an architecture's only heritable trait is its trainability, a single
entangled quantity with no separable sub-parts to recombine. Give the genome the
missing piece, \emph{weight inheritance}, so a candidate carries its trained modules
across generations, and crossover wins again by a wide margin. Crossover helps exactly
insofar as the genome carries separable, transferable building blocks. On a real NAS
benchmark (NAS-Bench-201) that rule is harder to see: the cell space is small and
crossover's benefit is tiny and not predicted by separability, a limit we report
honestly. \textbf{None of these mechanisms is new}: weight inheritance is the
Lamarckian evolution of LEMONADE \cite{lemonade2019}, modular crossover is that of He
et al.\ \cite{modxover2021}, and random search as a strong baseline is Li and Talwalkar
\cite{li2019}. The contribution is a single minimal dependency-free implementation that
reproduces and unifies them under one through-line with a trap-function positive
control, every figure regenerable from one command. It is a reproduction and synthesis,
offered as such.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Neural architecture search (NAS) automates the choice of a network's shape; methods
differ mainly in the search strategy, reinforcement learning \cite{zoph2017},
gradient relaxation \cite{darts2019}, Bayesian optimization, random search, and
evolution \cite{elsken2019}. Ours is evolutionary: the topology is evolved while each
candidate's weights are trained by back-propagation \cite{real2019}. The ordinary
signature \textbf{predict(topology, activation, \ldots)} becomes
\textbf{self\_modifying\_predict(data, error)}, and the rest is discovered. The engine
grew out of the author's 1997 seminar thesis, which derived the multi-layer delta rule
the training still uses \cite{thesis1997}.

A recurring result is that random search is hard to beat \cite{bergstra2012,li2019}.
We ask whether \emph{recombination} beats mutation at this search, and reach a clean
rule: crossover needs building blocks that carry a solution; standard NAS does not
provide them; weight inheritance does. The self-adaptive machinery is standard in the
evolution-strategy literature \cite{rechenberg1973}.

\section{Method}

A network is a feed-forward stack of dense and convolutional layers; $g$ is a
per-network hidden activation (sigmoid, $\tanh$, or ReLU) chosen by the genome, the
output a dense sigmoid. A convolutional layer applies $F$ shared kernels of width $K$,
so its width is derived, $d_\ell = F(d_{\ell-1}-K+1)$, the local weight-sharing bias
LeCun introduced \cite{lecun1989}, here a move the search can take. Weights train by
back-propagation with momentum (the 1997 thesis's delta rule \cite{thesis1997}),
initialized in $[-2.4/f_i,\,2.4/f_i]$; all arithmetic is 32-bit float, all randomness a
seeded xorshift. A \emph{genome} carries the topology, its training hyper-parameters,
and a self-adaptive mutation rate \cite{rechenberg1973}; one mutation perturbs, flips,
adds, or removes a layer. The search keeps a population, retains the best few, and
refills by reproduction, against a matched random-search control. Optional
\emph{crossover} (\texttt{evolve -X}) builds a child by a one-point splice of two
parents' hidden-layer lists plus a blend of hyper-parameters; with \texttt{-X 0} the
run is byte-identical to the mutation-only search.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{The operator works: a building-block control}

We first confirm the operator does what crossover should, on concatenated deceptive
trap functions: $M$ blocks of $K$ bits, a block scoring $K$ only if all its bits are
one and otherwise $K-1$ minus its number of ones, a deceptive gradient toward all-zero.
The optimum is reachable only by assembling solved blocks. We race the same GA with
one-point crossover against mutation-only over 200 seeds (\texttt{make bbtest}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Crossover versus mutation on concatenated deceptive traps ($K=4$, population
1000, tournament 3, 200 seeds). Crossover's advantage grows with the number of blocks;
by sixteen, mutation mostly fails while crossover still solves.}
\label{tab:bb}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
blocks $M$ & mutation-only & crossover $+$ mutation & speedup \\
\midrule
4  & 9.2 gens, 200/200  & 5.1 gens, 200/200  & $1.8\times$ \\
8  & 70.8 gens, 200/200 & 14.2 gens, 200/200 & $5\times$ \\
12 & 257 gens, 185/200  & 22.6 gens, 200/200 & $11\times$ \\
16 & 363 gens, \textbf{28/200} & 42.3 gens, \textbf{199/200} & mutation fails \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The operator is sound: when separable building blocks exist, crossover finds and
combines them, and its advantage scales with the amount of structure (Table~\ref{tab:bb}).

\subsection{On the architecture search, crossover does not help, and why}

We run the same on/off comparison on the neural search: for each seed a fresh synthetic
task ($d=8$, a random-projection sine label), the search run twice from an identical
initial population, mutation-only and with crossover, to a target error. The two are
indistinguishable; adding recombination does nothing, and not because the operator is
weak (Table~\ref{tab:bb}). Two measurements say why. Architecture alone does not solve
the task, every topology scores at or below chance ($0.25$--$0.59$ test-MSE) with
default hyper-parameters; the \emph{hyper-parameters} do the work, and brittly, holding
a $8,4,1$ topology and varying only them swings the error from $0.09$ to $0.65$, while
with good hyper-parameters fixed the topology matters narrowly and non-monotonically
($8,4,1 \to 0.09$, $8,8,1 \to 0.37$). A parallel-branch probe (\texttt{make modnas},
gradient-checked) adds that even a purpose-built modular architecture does not
decompose, because neural capacity is fungible: one wide branch does what several
specialised ones would. So the architecture has a heritable trait, its trainability,
but that trait is a single entangled quantity with no independent sub-parts; there are
no building blocks to recombine, even though there is something to inherit.

\subsection{Weight inheritance restores the building blocks}

The fix is to let the genome carry the learned state, not only the shape. We test it in
a modular setting (\texttt{make modevo}, gradient-checked): a candidate is $G$
independent sub-networks, one per output component, each a hard sine of its own input
group so that training from a random start succeeds only sometimes. A candidate
\emph{carries its sub-networks' trained weights across generations}; crossover
recombines whole solved sub-networks from two parents, while mutation must re-roll the
initialization lottery for a stuck component within one lineage.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Weight-inheriting crossover versus mutation on the modular task (population 30,
300 fine-tune steps/generation, 60 seeds, cap 120 generations). Crossover solves every
task in about twelve generations whatever the count; mutation is several times slower
and, as components accumulate, often fails.}
\label{tab:modevo}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
components $G$ & mutation (re-init) & crossover (inherit) \\
\midrule
2 & 60/60, 31.0 gens & 60/60, \textbf{11.4} gens \\
3 & \textbf{20/60}, 47.9 gens & 60/60, \textbf{12.1} gens \\
4 & 59/60, 84.6 gens & 60/60, \textbf{12.6} gens \\
5 & 59/60, 39.4 gens & 60/60, \textbf{12.5} gens \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

With weights inherited (Table~\ref{tab:modevo}) crossover wins again, solving $60/60$ in
roughly twelve generations independent of the count while mutation is $2.7$ to
$6.7\times$ slower and unreliable. The recombination that did nothing to an
architecture-only genome is decisive once the genome carries the solution, exactly as
on the trap bits.

Wired through the full engine (\texttt{evolve -w}), a checkpoint of each candidate's
trained weights crosses the worker boundary and a child warm-starts from its parent
over the layers a mutation left unchanged. A $300$-seed-per-cell sweep
(\texttt{scripts/inherit\_sweep.sh}) shows inheritance lowers the search's final error
at equal compute in every cell (exact sign test $p<10^{-3}$), by a relative $21\%$ at a
$20$-epoch budget down to $6\%$ at $160$: warm-starting helps most when training is
scarce and there is discarded training to reclaim, and fades as candidates converge on
their own. With inheritance off the run is byte-identical to before, and the random
control, having no lineage, is identical in both arms.

\subsection{Where architecture is decisive, the search finds convolution}

Our tasks make architecture fungible; the opposite regime exists. On a
translation-invariant image task (\texttt{make conv2d}, a $3\times3$ diagonal motif at a
random position in a noisy $8\times8$ image, label its orientation) a 2D convolution
that detects the motif anywhere reaches $0.1\%$ test error from $2000$ images while a
same-size dense network is near chance ($39\%$), catching up only with an order of
magnitude more data. Here the right structure carries the solution. Giving the search a
native 2D convolution front-end ($F$ filters of $K\times K$ with global max-pool,
\texttt{conv2f}, gradient-checked) and running it on this task
(\texttt{gentask -I 8}), over $40$ seeds it discovers the convolution in \emph{every
one}, reaching a median test-MSE of $2\times10^{-7}$, five orders of magnitude below the
dense architectures it can equally build. On the fungible tasks it never preferred
convolution; given a task where structure is decisive and reachable, the search finds
the local weight-sharing bias LeCun designed by hand.

\subsection{On a real benchmark, the rule is harder to see}

The trap control is clean by construction; a real NAS space is not. On NAS-Bench-201
\cite{li2019} (its topology cell, $15{,}625$ architectures with precomputed
CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet16 accuracies, so fitness is a table lookup, \texttt{make
nasxover}) crossover yields only a small early-convergence gain over mutation (at most a
few tenths of a percent accuracy, and none at a generous budget, both operators nearly
reaching the optimum because the cell has only six genes). More tellingly, additive
separability does \emph{not} predict crossover's benefit: over $120$ sub-spaces per
dataset (fixed size, varying which operations are allowed) the correlation between the
additive-model $R^2$ and crossover's benefit is null ($r = -0.06, -0.03, -0.11$, none
significant). The building-block rule is clean on designed problems but does not
straightforwardly transfer to real NAS cell spaces, which are small and do not decompose
in a way separability captures. We report this rather than tune a predictor until one
correlates.

\section{Relation to prior work}

None of the mechanisms here is new. Random search as a strong NAS baseline is
\cite{bergstra2012,li2019}; mutation-only evolutionary NAS with aging is \cite{real2019};
weight inheritance, a child warm-started from its trained parent, is exactly the
Lamarckian inheritance of LEMONADE \cite{lemonade2019}, built on the function-preserving
transfer of Net2Net \cite{net2net2016} and Network Morphism \cite{netmorph2016} and
related to ENAS parameter sharing \cite{enas2018}; crossover that inherits trained
modules is He et al.'s modular inheritable crossover \cite{modxover2021}; and the
alignment that makes topology crossover meaningful is NEAT's historical marking
\cite{neat2002}. This work contributes no method beyond these. It offers a single,
minimal, dependency-free implementation that reproduces and unifies them under one
through-line, recombination helps insofar as the genome carries separable transferable
building blocks, with a trap-function positive control (Table~\ref{tab:bb}) isolating
that condition and every figure regenerable from one command. It is a reproduction and
synthesis, not a discovery.

\section{Discussion and conclusion}

The experiments compose into one rule: crossover is exactly as useful as the genome is a
carrier of separable building blocks. An architecture genome carries only trainability, a
single entangled quantity, so recombination has nothing to combine, which is a
structural reason random search is strong in NAS and the field \cite{real2019} drops
crossover; weight inheritance gives the genome a second, decomposable thing to carry, the
trained solution, and the advantage returns (Table~\ref{tab:modevo}). The same search,
given a decisive and reachable structure, rediscovers 2D convolution on an image task
unaided. On a real tabular benchmark the rule is muted, its cell space too small and too
entangled for separability to bite. \textbf{Limitations:} scale is small and tasks are
mostly synthetic, mean squared error stands in for classification error, and the
building-block predictor does not carry to real NAS (Section 3.5), all reported openly.
The natural next step is the same comparison on larger, hierarchical search spaces where
recombination has more room to matter.

\section*{Reproducibility}

Every result regenerates from \cite{repo} with one command. The engine builds with
\texttt{make} (C99, no dependencies) into \texttt{smbpann}, \texttt{evolve} (with
\texttt{-X} crossover and \texttt{-w} weight inheritance), and \texttt{gentask};
\texttt{make ut} runs the unit and gradient-check suite under AddressSanitizer and UBSan.
Standalone probes: \texttt{make bbtest} (Table~\ref{tab:bb}), \texttt{make modnas},
\texttt{make modevo} (Table~\ref{tab:modevo}), \texttt{make conv2d}, and
\texttt{make nasxover} (Section 3.5). Search A/Bs are \texttt{scripts/xover\_ab.sh},
\texttt{scripts/inherit\_ab.sh}, and \texttt{scripts/inherit\_sweep.sh}. Each run uses a
fixed 32-bit xorshift generator, so the numbers reproduce exactly.
